Mattia Almansi

Ocean Modeller

National Oceanography Centre

Hi there! My name is Mattia and I’m an ocean modeller at the National Oceanography Centre in Southampton. I’m primarily interested in the physical processes governing the overflows of deep water masses between ocean basins. To address the targets of my research, I run high-resolution numerical simulations and I combine model output with available observations.

During my PhD at Johns Hopkins University, I studied the dynamics controlling the variability of the circulation in the vicinity of Denmark Strait, advised by Prof. Tom Haine.

Projects

Denmark Strait

Ocean currents affecting the global climate are sustained by cold and dense water that sinks in the North Atlantic Ocean. A large portion of this water passes through Denmark Strait, the ocean channel located between Greenland and Iceland. The amount of water entering the strait varies from day to day, and is controlled by ocean vortices. Knowing the mechanisms associated with these vortices is of key importance for understanding and predicting Earth’s climate.

Selected Publications

M. Almansi, T.W.N. Haine, R. Gelderloos, R.S. Pickart
January 2020 Geophysical Research Letters

Evolution of Denmark Strait Overflow Cyclones and Their Relationship to Overflow Surges

Mesoscale features present at the Denmark Strait sill regularly enhance the volume transport of the Denmark Strait Overflow (DSO). They are important for the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, and ultimately, for the global climate system. Using a realistic numerical model, we find new evidence of the causal relationship between overflow surges (i.e., mesoscale features associated with high-transport events) and DSO cyclones observed downstream. Most of the cyclones form at the Denmark Strait sill during overflow surges and, because of potential vorticity conservation and stretching of the water column, grow as they move equatorward. A fraction of the cyclones form downstream of the sill, when anticyclonic vortices formed during high-transport events start collapsing. Regardless of their formation mechanism, DSO cyclones weaken starting roughly 150 km downstream of the sill, and potential vorticity is only materially conserved during the growth phase.
